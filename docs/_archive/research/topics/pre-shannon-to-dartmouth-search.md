## Answer

Here’s a compact, date-anchored overview that leads up to McCulloch–Pitts (1943) and the 1956 Dartmouth conference, with Claude Shannon’s role called out.

Pre-1943 foundations (before McCulloch–Pitts)

- Ancient to early modern automata
  - ca. 1st c. CE: Hero of Alexandria’s mechanical automata.
  - 1206: Al‑Jazari’s programmable water clocks/automata.
  - 1738–1739: Vaucanson’s automata (e.g., the “digesting duck”).
- Mechanization of calculation
  - 1642: Pascal’s Pascaline.
  - 1673: Leibniz’s Stepped Reckoner; 1703: Leibniz on binary arithmetic.
  - 1804–1805: Jacquard loom (punched cards; programmability).
  - 1837–1843: Babbage’s Analytical Engine; Ada Lovelace’s 1843 Notes (algorithm idea).
  - 1890: Hollerith tabulators for the U.S. Census.
- Formal logic and the dream of mechanical reasoning
  - 1847/1854: Boole’s algebra of logic.
  - 1879: Frege’s Begriffsschrift (formal logic).
  - 1910–1913: Russell & Whitehead’s Principia Mathematica.
  - 1928–1936: Hilbert’s Entscheidungsproblem; 1931: Gödel’s incompleteness; 1936: Church’s λ‑calculus and Turing’s universal machine.
- Neuroscience and mathematical biophysics (toward neural models)
  - 1890s: Ramón y Cajal (neuron doctrine); 1897: Sherrington (synapse).
  - 1907: Lapicque’s integrate‑and‑fire neuron model.
  - 1930s: Rashevsky’s mathematical biophysics of neural nets.
- Control and feedback (proto‑cybernetics)
  - 1868: Maxwell on governors (feedback control).
  - 1927–1934: Black’s negative‑feedback amplifiers; emerging servomechanisms.
  - 1931–1936: Vannevar Bush’s Differential Analyzer (analog computing).
- Early digital machines
  - 1938–1941: Zuse’s Z1–Z3 (binary, electromechanical).
  - 1939–1942: Atanasoff–Berry Computer (ABC, electronic, special‑purpose).
- Claude Shannon before 1943
  - 1937: Master’s thesis unifying Boolean logic with relay/switching circuits (foundation for digital logic design).
  - 1938: Paper on symbolic analysis of relay circuits expands the program.

The 1943 milestone: McCulloch–Pitts

- Paper: “A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity” (1943).
- Key ideas:
  - Simple threshold neurons can implement propositional logic.
  - Networks of such neurons are, in principle, computationally universal.
  - Time and connectivity matter: they analyze nets, not just single units.
- Impact:
  - Launched neural network theory and connected neurobiology to computation.
  - Inspired later learning models (Hebb 1949 is soon after) and cybernetics.

Between 1943 and Dartmouth (setting the AI stage)

- 1943–1945: Colossus (codebreaking) and wartime computing show practical power.
- 1945: Vannevar Bush, “As We May Think,” vision of memex and augmented intellect.
- 1948: Shannon’s “A Mathematical Theory of Communication” (information theory).
- 1948: Wiener’s “Cybernetics” frames feedback, control, and communication in animals and machines.
- 1950: Turing’s “Computing Machinery and Intelligence” and the imitation game test.
- 1950: Shannon’s “Programming a Computer for Playing Chess” (game‑tree search, evaluation functions); 1950–1951: his Theseus maze‑solving mouse (learning/maze memory demo).
- Early 1950s: First stored‑program computers and experiments in checkers/chess, theorem proving, and simple learning circuits.

Dartmouth Conference (Summer 1956)

- Organizers/proposers: John McCarthy (lead), Marvin Minsky, Nathaniel Rochester, and Claude Shannon.
- Aim: a summer project to explore how “every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it.”
- Lasting effects:
  - Coined and consolidated the term “Artificial Intelligence.”
  - Seeded seminal strands: symbolic reasoning, search, heuristics, game playing, and early learning ideas.
  - Drew together communities from logic, cybernetics, information theory, and computing.

Claude Shannon’s specific role

- Pre‑AI digital foundations: 1937 Boolean‑circuit thesis enabled reliable digital computers.
- Information theory (1948): rigorous concepts of information, entropy, and channel capacity that later underpinned learning, coding, pattern recognition, and communication in intelligent systems.
- Early AI methods: 1950 chess paper introducing game‑tree search with evaluation; emphasis on heuristics.
- Embodied demonstrations: Theseus (1950–1951), a hardware “learning” system navigating mazes.
- Community builder: co‑proposer of the 1956 Dartmouth project, helping define AI as a coherent research agenda.
