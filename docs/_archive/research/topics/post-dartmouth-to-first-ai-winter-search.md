## Answer

Here’s a concise, connected sketch of the early (symbolic) AI story around your keywords.

At a glance timeline (1950s–early 1980s)

- 1956: Dartmouth workshop coins “artificial intelligence.” Early work focuses on symbolic reasoning, logic, and search (later nicknamed GOFAI).
- 1956–1959: Symbolic problem solving
  - Logic Theorist (Newell & Simon, 1956) proves propositional logic theorems.
  - General Problem Solver/GPS (Newell, Shaw, Simon, 1957–59) explores heuristic search over symbol structures.
- 1958–1972: Key languages and formalisms
  - LISP (McCarthy, 1958/60) becomes AI’s lingua franca.
  - STRIPS planning (Fikes & Nilsson, 1971) for action and planning.
  - Prolog and logic programming (Colmerauer; Kowalski, 1972) link computation with automated reasoning.
- 1959–1965: Automated theorem proving (ATP)
  - Early SAT/first-order methods: Davis–Putnam (1960), DPLL (Davis–Logemann–Loveland, 1962).
  - Resolution and unification (Robinson, 1965) make refutation-based ATP practical; foundations for later model checking and logic programming.
- 1966–1972: Shakey the Robot (SRI) integrates perception, planning (STRIPS), and action—impressive demos but reveals scaling limits.
- 1965–early 1980s: Expert systems (knowledge-based AI)
  - DENDRAL (Feigenbaum, Buchanan, Lederberg, mid‑1960s) infers molecular structure from mass spectra.
  - MYCIN (Shortliffe, 1972–76) recommends antibiotics for blood infections; famous for its rule base and explanation facility (not deployed clinically).
  - XCON/R1 (DEC, 1978–80) configures VAX systems; a flagship commercial success that fuels the 1980s expert‑systems boom.

Why the first AI winter happened (roughly 1974–1980)

- The Lighthill report (UK, 1973): Sir James Lighthill’s review criticized most AI (especially general-purpose “Category B” research) for overpromising and running into combinatorial explosion. UK government sharply reduced funding; several university groups were curtailed or closed.
- DARPA/US funding retrenchment (early–mid 1970s): After ambitious, under‑delivering programs—most notably the Speech Understanding Research program (c. 1971–74)—DARPA scaled back open‑ended AI funding and pushed for defense-relevant deliverables. Earlier, the ALPAC report (1966) had already collapsed U.S. machine translation funding.
- Technical headwinds: Limited compute/memory, brittle symbolic representations, weak perception/learning, and difficulty integrating vision, language, and reasoning at scale.
  Net effect: By 1974 funding and optimism dipped; many projects narrowed scope. The downturn eased later in the decade as expert systems showed focused, domain‑specific value, setting up the 1980s boom (and, later, the second winter after 1987).

How the pieces connect

- Symbolic AI provided the representation and inference machinery (LISP, production rules, logic).
- Theorem proving and SAT/first-order methods supplied core reasoning engines (resolution, unification, DPLL) that later powered Prolog, verification, and constraint reasoning.
- Expert systems converted symbolic know‑how into practical, narrow applications, reviving industry confidence after the first winter.
- Lighthill (1973) and DARPA cutbacks crystallized limits of general, open‑ended symbolic AI, pushing the field toward narrower, knowledge‑rich systems and, eventually, toward data‑driven methods.
