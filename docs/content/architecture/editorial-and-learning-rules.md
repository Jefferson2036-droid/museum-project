# Editorial And Learning Rules

## Purpose

These rules translate the feature spec's editorial and cognitive-load goals into
practical guidance for content implementation.

## Reader Model

The reader is intelligent but not specialized.

That means the content should:

- assume curiosity, not prior expertise
- introduce technical terms only when needed
- explain difficult transitions instead of skipping over them
- preserve nuance without burying the reader in specialist shorthand

## Segmentation Rules

- organize long material into short sections built around one main idea
- prefer strong headings over long uninterrupted exposition
- use short explanatory transitions between major historical jumps
- keep lists and side material purposeful rather than decorative

## Chronology And Concept Balance

- chronology remains the primary teaching spine
- concept teaching should interrupt the chronology only when it removes real
  confusion
- if a technical concept is essential to understanding an event, explain it at
  the moment of need and then continue the story
- deeper concept treatment may live in a linked concept unit instead of bloating
  the era narrative

## Technical Concept Introduction Rules

- define a concept in plain language first
- show why it mattered in its original historical moment
- only then connect it to modern AI systems
- avoid teaching modern terms as if they existed unchanged in earlier periods

## Guide Commentary Density

- use guide commentary sparingly
- one strong callout is better than several weak ones
- insert a guide callout only when it reduces friction, clarifies significance,
  or blocks a likely misconception

## Media Use Rules

- media should support comprehension, not decorate the page
- diagrams are preferred when they clarify sequence, causality, or structure
- sourced images should add historical specificity, not generic atmosphere
- audio planning should focus on segments that help readers who benefit from a
  narrative explanation of chronology or significance

## Swiss-Style Presentation Guidance

- use strong visual hierarchy
- keep layouts clean and ordered
- prefer clarity, spacing, and contrast over ornamental effects
- let typography and structure do most of the work of orientation

## Engagement Framework Translation

- use the Sage posture to favor explanation, orientation, and intellectual
  honesty over performance or hype
- use the Explorer posture to preserve discovery, curiosity, and the sense that
  ideas emerge through contact with real historical terrain
- use Cialdini-style sequencing only as an educational ordering tool: place the
  most clarifying example or consequence where it earns attention, then support
  it with evidence and context
- do not use persuasion techniques to pressure agreement, inflate certainty, or
  turn unresolved historical questions into smooth marketing copy
- when these frameworks influence a page, that influence should show up as
  pacing, emphasis, and reader orientation rather than named branding language

## Human-Centered Framing Rules

- keep people, institutions, rivalries, and constraints visible
- do not tell the story as if progress were automatic or inevitable
- make it clear where the field changed because of choices, funding, criticism,
  or failure

## Language Rules

- serious but accessible tone
- avoid hype phrasing
- avoid flattening disagreement into false certainty
- avoid persuasive shortcuts that sacrifice accuracy for momentum

## Implementation Use

The current live exemplar and future expansion work should use these rules to
shape:

- section lengths and sequence
- guide-callout placement
- concept explainer placement
- diagram selection
- audio segment choice
