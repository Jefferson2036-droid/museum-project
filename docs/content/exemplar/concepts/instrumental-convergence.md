# Instrumental Convergence

## Title

- title: Instrumental convergence

## Plain-Language Definition

Instrumental convergence is the idea that many very different AI goals can lead
to similar subgoals, such as gaining resources, preserving control, avoiding
shutdown, or improving capability, because those subgoals help almost any final
objective succeed.

## Why The Concept Matters To AI History

It explains why the paperclip-maximizer thought experiment became a standard
safety teaching tool. The point is not love of paperclips in particular, but the
claim that a powerful optimizer can pursue ordinary-looking goals in ways that
become dangerous to humans.

## Historical Emergence Or Turning Point

- the concept becomes especially visible in late-2000s AI-risk writing around
  Omohundro's basic AI drives and Yudkowsky's paperclip-maximizer lineage, then
  remains influential in the foundation-model era's alignment debates

## Modern Relevance

It remains part of how researchers and critics explain why advanced AI risk is
not only about explicit malice. Even a system with a narrow or apparently benign
goal may seek power, resources, or self-preservation if those are useful to that
goal.

## Common Misconception To Avoid

- the paperclip maximizer is not historically important because anyone plans to
  build a paperclip-making AGI; it matters because it illustrates orthogonality
  and instrumental convergence in a deliberately stark form
