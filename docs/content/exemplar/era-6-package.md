## One-Sentence Thesis

AI's modern performance surge comes when deep neural architectures, learned
representations, large-scale training, and finally transformers reshape what the
field can do in vision, speech, and language.

## Why This Era Matters

- it explains why deep learning becomes the dominant technical story before the
  foundation-model era
- it shows that the crucial change is not merely "neural nets exist" but that
  representation learning at scale starts winning benchmark and product-level
  contests
- it introduces transformers as a historically situated turning point rather
  than a detached buzzword

## Core Narrative

Era 6 should be taught as the period when deep learning stops being a promising
direction and becomes the field's central performance regime. The key difference
from Era 5 is scale and consequence. Large datasets, improved hardware, and
better training methods let researchers learn multi-layer representations that
produce major gains in image recognition, speech recognition, and later natural
language processing.

Representation learning is the core teaching concept here. Instead of relying on
people to hand-engineer every useful feature, deep systems increasingly learn
intermediate structures for themselves. That shift changes not only benchmark
results but also the way the field imagines future progress. The transformer then
marks the late-Era-6 turning point because it shows that sequence modeling can be
rebuilt around attention, greater parallelism, and cleaner scaling. That is the
direct runway into the foundation-model and large-language-model era.

## Transition In And Transition Out

- transition in:
  Era 5 establishes the statistical turn and renewed neural credibility; Era 6
  begins when layered representation learning at scale becomes the strongest
  performance story across multiple domains.
- transition out:
  Era 7 begins when transformers and large-scale training open the door to
  foundation models, large language models, and the public generative-AI period.
