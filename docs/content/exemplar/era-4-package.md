- date range: 1970-1987

## One-Sentence Thesis

AI's first major contraction came when expert systems proved narrow value even
as broader symbolic ambitions ran into critique, scaling limits, and tightening
institutional expectations.

## Why This Era Matters

- it prevents the history from becoming a smooth progress story
- it shows why expert systems counted as genuine achievement even inside a
  period of disappointment
- it gives later statistical and neural eras a clear contrast case for what
  symbolic methods could and could not do well

## Core Narrative

Era 4 should be taught as a period of narrowing rather than simple collapse.
Symbolic AI did not disappear after the early optimism of the 1950s and 1960s.
Instead, it concentrated its strength in systems that worked best when expertise
could be explicitly represented, rules could be carefully engineered, and the
task domain remained bounded. DENDRAL and MYCIN are the canonical examples,
because they make the case that symbolic methods could produce serious results
when the problem was framed tightly enough.

At the same time, the era makes clear why that success did not generalize
quickly. Knowledge engineering was expensive, symbolic systems were brittle when
contexts changed, and broad claims about common-sense reasoning or general
robotic intelligence met technical and institutional skepticism. The Lighthill
report becomes a turning point not because it single-handedly caused the winter,
but because it made visible a larger mismatch between ambition, scaling, and
funding tolerance.

## Transition In And Transition Out

- transition in:
  Era 3 builds confidence in symbolic reasoning and planning; Era 4 shows what
  happens when that confidence is pushed into harder domains and funders demand
  more concrete returns.
- transition out:
  Era 5 begins when AI's center of gravity shifts toward statistical learning,
  larger datasets, and renewed neural credibility after the limits of hand-built
  symbolic knowledge become hard to ignore.
